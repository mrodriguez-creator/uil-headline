What is UIL Headline Writing?

UIL Headline Writing is a contest where you read short news stories and write headlines that summarize the main idea in a limited number of characters. You'll write 6 headlines in 30 minutes!

The Goal:

Write a headline that tells the story and sells the story - it should be accurate, attention-grabbing, and follow UIL rules.

The 10 Essential Rules

1 Active Voice Only

‚úì GOOD: "Student wins scholarship"

‚úó BAD: "Scholarship won by student"

2 Present or Future Tense (Never Past)

‚úì GOOD: "Board approves new policy"

‚úó BAD: "Board approved new policy"

3 Stay Within Character Count

Every character counts! Going over or under = disqualification for that headline.

4 No Articles (a, an, the)

‚úì GOOD: "School adds robotics class"

‚úó BAD: "The school adds a robotics class"

5 Use Last Names Only

‚úì GOOD: "Johnson earns award"

‚úó BAD: "Sarah Johnson earns award" or "Sarah earns award"

6 Multi-line Headlines = One Sentence

If you have 2 or 3 lines, they must read as one complete sentence from top to bottom.

7 Don't Split Verb Phrases Between Lines

‚úó BAD:
Line 1: "Students plan to"
Line 2: "attend college fair"

‚úì GOOD:
Line 1: "Students plan"
Line 2: "college fair visit"

8 Grab Main Idea from Lead (First 2 Paragraphs)

Focus on the most important info at the beginning of the story, not minor details from later paragraphs.

9 No Abbreviations (Unless in Story)

‚úó BAD: "NHS inducts 20 students" (unless NHS is spelled out in story)

‚úì GOOD: "Honor society inducts 20"

10 Use Strong, Specific Verbs

‚úì GOOD: "Team captures championship"

‚úó WEAK: "Team gets championship"

How Headlines Are Scored (0-4 Points)

Score What It Means
0 Points Disqualified - over/under character count
1 Point Major errors: passive voice, past tense, misspellings, using articles, wrong names
2 Points Misses main idea, uses first names, bad word splits, double quotes
3 Points Good headline - summarizes story, correct grammar, not outstanding
4 Points Excellent - nails the lead, strong verb, clever, every word counts!

Step-by-Step: How to Write a Headline

"We've worked so hard all year for this opportunity," team captain Maria Santos said. "Winning regionals showed us we can compete with anyone."

The team earned first place in the programming category and second place overall at the regional competition in Austin. They will face 32 teams at the state championship in Dallas.

1 Identify the Main Idea

What's the MOST important news? ‚Üí Team going to state championship

2 Find the Key Facts
  • Who: Robotics team
  • What: Will compete / advance
  • Where: State championship
  • Why/How: Won regionals
3 Choose a Strong Verb (Present/Future Tense)

‚úì Good verbs: advance, compete, qualify, earn, capture

‚úó Avoid: won (past tense), will be competing (wordy)

4 Write Subject + Verb + Object

Draft: "Robotics team advances to state championship"

5 Count Characters & Adjust

"Robotics team advances to state championship" = 46 characters

If limit is 30: "Robotics team earns state bid" = 31 characters (still too long!)

Final: "Robotics earns state berth" = 28 characters ‚úì

6 Check All Rules
  • ‚úì Active voice
  • ‚úì Present tense
  • ‚úì Within character count
  • ‚úì No articles
  • ‚úì Strong verb
  • ‚úì Captures main idea
